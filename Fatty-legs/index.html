Introduction to Olemaun

Fatty Legs protagonist Olemaun is brave, determined. Yet, her story is one of resilience and self discovering; of holding onto her identity, while maintaining a very real connection to the harsh realities of a residential school.

Strength and Determination

The thing that makes Olemaun so strong is that she is determined to learn to read. The rules of the school are strict and often cruel, but her passion for knowledge compels her to continue. She’s brave when she’s humiliated and she faces challenges, but still she’s not willing to give up on her goals.

Introduction to themes

Living a life full of crucial events, fears and hopes the novel Fatty Legs raises important topics that define the experience of Olemaun and such themes as cultural preservation, personal and community strength and individuality. These themes are present throughout the story to inform readers that there is power necessary to be oneself in a society that seeks to transform people.

Cultural Suppresion

Fatty Legs is a book that has a major theme of the residential school suppressing Olemaun’s Inuit culture. They thrust her from her language and her traditions and her identity and substitute European customs. This theme is about the catastrophic consequences of the loss of culture and the significance of preserving one’s heritage.

Power Of Education

The love of learning is what motivates Olemaun to the residential school. But the challenges are there; she learns, and the learning becomes a sort of empowerment. This theme highlights education as a complex force: it can be either a means of oppression or a means to personal freedom.

Identity And Self Discovery

Olemaun’s journey is a journey of self discovery of how to continue to be who she is even when the school tries to ‘strip’ her of her identity. Red stockings become her way to create her own individuality; she was never going to be able to fully deny who she really is despite the pressure to conform.

3.

Symbols

Symbols in Fatty Legs point out the resilience and strong sense of individuality in Olemaun. The red stockings symbolize this immensely; intended to humiliate her, they become proud symbols in their own right of her bravery and refusal to give in. Its bright red screams out her insistence on holding on to her identity against the school's harsh rules. Another important symbol, the raven, links her to Inuit heritage and signifies wisdom and resilience. Together, these symbols encapsulate the inner strength of Olemaun and show readers the importance of being true to oneself in the face of adversity.
